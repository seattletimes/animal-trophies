Threatened by trade

Thousands of plant and animal species are protected under an international treaty known as CITES. Unlike the U.S. Endangered Species Act, which focuses on conservation, CITES protects threatened species through regulation of international trade.

27% of shipments through the Port of Seattle in 2014 contained at least one animal currently classified as in danger of extinction (CITES Appendix I) or in danger of extinction if trade is not controlled (CITES Appendix II).

On display

Many wildlife shipments include animal parts and products, such as meat, leather or shell jewelry. However, other shipments include whole game animals hunted for sport or food and intended for display.

Last year, 24% of shipments through the Port of Seattle contained at least one of these "trophy" animals. Most trophy animals were hunted in Africa.

Hunting the protected

Animals protected by CITES can still be imported legally with proper documentation.

Of the trophy animals shipped through the Port of Seattle last year, 63% were protected by CITES Appendices I and II.

New statewide legislation

This November, Washington voters will consider an intiative banning most sales of the following animals (including parts and products): elephants, rhinos, lions, tigers, leopards, cheetahs, pangolins, marine turtles, sharks and rays.

Last year, 4% of shipments through the Port of Seattle contained at least one of these animals.
