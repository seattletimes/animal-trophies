Threatened by trade

An international treaty called CITES protects thousands of threatened animals and plants by regulating their trade.

Last year, 27% of wildlife shipments through the Port of Seattle included at least one animal considered to be in danger of extinction (CITES Appendix I) or in danger of extinction if trade is not controlled (CITES Appendix II).

On display

Many wildlife shipments include animal parts and products, such as meat, leather or shell jewelry. However, other shipments include trophy animals — whole game animals hunted and intended for display.

Last year, 24% of wildlife shipments through the Port of Seattle contained at least one trophy animal. Most were hunted in Africa.

Hunting the protected

Of the trophy animals shipped through the Port of Seattle last year, 63% were protected by the CITES treaty and allowed to be legally imported with proper documentation.

Proposed statewide legislation

This November, Washington voters will consider an initiative (I-1401) banning most sales of the following animals (including parts and products): elephants, rhinos, lions, tigers, leopards, cheetahs, pangolins, marine turtles, sharks and rays.

Last year, 4% of wildlife shipments through the Port of Seattle contained at least one of these animals.
